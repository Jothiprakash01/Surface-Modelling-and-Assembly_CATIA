TITLE OF THE PRODUCT
Design, Structural Optimization, and Finite Element Analysis of a Compact Aluminium Linkage Bracket

DESCRIPTION
Abstract
The Compact Aluminium Linkage Bracket is a lightweight mechanical component designed to transmit motion, force, and moment loads between interconnected members in a reciprocating mechanism. The objective of this project was to design, model, simulate, and validate a structurally efficient linkage bracket capable of operating under combined gravitational and torque loading conditions. The component was developed using Aluminium Alloy 1060 due to its excellent strength-to-weight ratio, corrosion resistance, and manufacturability. A detailed three-dimensional solid model was created incorporating dual pivot bores, a contoured connecting web, and filleted transitions to improve structural performance. Finite Element Analysis (FEA) was performed under realistic operating conditions including a fixed pivot support, gravitational loading, and a remote moment load. The simulation results indicated a maximum deformation of 0.1247 mm and a peak von Mises stress of approximately 39 MPa. The study successfully identified critical stress regions and proposed design improvements to enhance the factor of safety. The project demonstrates a complete product development cycle from concept generation and CAD modeling to structural analysis and prototype preparation.

Introduction
Linkage mechanisms are fundamental components of many engineering systems where rotary motion must be converted into reciprocating or oscillatory motion. The performance and reliability of such mechanisms depend significantly on the strength and stiffness of the connecting elements that transfer loads between pivots. Among these components, linkage brackets play a crucial role by transmitting forces, moments, and motion while maintaining geometric alignment throughout operation.

Modern mechanical systems demand components that are lightweight, compact, and capable of withstanding complex loading conditions without excessive deformation. To meet these requirements, engineers employ computer-aided design and simulation tools to optimize component geometry before manufacturing.

This project focuses on the design, modeling, and simulation of a compact aluminium linkage bracket intended for small reciprocating mechanisms. The study incorporates material selection, geometric optimization, finite element analysis, and prototype planning to ensure structural reliability and manufacturability. The results provide valuable insights into the behaviour of linkage components under torsional and bending loads and establish a foundation for future design improvements.

Working Principle
The Linkage Bracket is a dual-pivot mechanical component consisting of two cylindrical bosses connected through a contoured structural web. The larger bore serves as the fixed pivot interface, while the smaller bore functions as the moving pivot through which external loads and moments are introduced into the component.

The working principle of the linkage bracket is based on force and motion transmission between two pivot points. During operation, one end of the bracket remains constrained while the other experiences rotational movement and torque transfer. As loads are applied, the connecting web transfers stresses between the two pivot regions while maintaining alignment and minimizing deformation.

The component experiences a combination of bending, torsional, and shear stresses generated by the applied remote moment and gravitational loading. To improve load distribution, fillets are incorporated at the boss-to-web transitions, reducing stress concentrations and improving fatigue resistance. The lightweight aluminium construction ensures efficient operation while minimizing inertial effects within the reciprocating mechanism.

Detailed Applications of Mounting Support Bracket
Linkage brackets are widely used in mechanical systems that require controlled transmission of force and motion between connected members. Typical applications include:

Crank-slider mechanisms used in compressors and pumps.

Reciprocating engine linkages and valve actuation systems.

Mechanical actuators and motion control assemblies.

Folding and deployment mechanisms in consumer products.

Robotic arms and articulated motion systems.

Automotive pedal and suspension linkage assemblies.

Industrial machinery employing oscillatory motion transfer.

CHALLENGES FACED TO DEVELOP
The development of the linkage bracket involved several design and engineering challenges:

Mass vs. Strength Optimization: One of the primary difficulties was maintaining adequate strength around the pivot bores while minimizing overall mass. Since the component is subjected to torque transfer, stress concentrations naturally develop around the bore regions and geometric transitions.

Web Profile Sculpting: Another challenge involved optimizing the web profile between the two bosses. Excessive material increased weight, whereas insufficient material resulted in higher stress levels and reduced stiffness. Designing smooth, aerodynamic transitions and fillets required multiple iterations to achieve an optimal balance between structural performance and manufacturability.

SURFACE MODELS
The geometrical layout of the bracket was executed using advanced surface and solid modeling strategies to obtain a smooth, organic shape. High-quality surface boundaries were constructed using spline curves to establish an aerodynamic web profile connecting the two primary hubs.Tangency ($G^1$) and Curvature ($G^2$) continuity conditions were maintained along the transition zones where the web connects to the cylindrical pivot housings. This smooth topological blending was critical to preventing sudden geometrical transitions, ensuring that the final component remains free of surface defects and aesthetic irregularities before being thickened into a solid part.

ASSEMBLY MODEL
To guarantee proper functional integration within a larger reciprocating mechanism, appropriate assembly constraints and structural alignments were defined. The larger bore was designated as the primary reference hub, establishing a rigid coaxial alignment with the main support pivot. The smaller bore was aligned to maintain secondary kinematics for motion transmission.

Interference and clearance checks were conducted across the interface boundaries to avoid mechanical conflict. Appropriate clearance values were incorporated into the design to accommodate structural pins and rotational bearings, confirming a zero-interference fit across all mating components.

Meshing Strategy and Discretization
To perform a highly reliable structural computation, the 3D solid geometry was discretized using an unstructured spatial continuum mesh. Solid continuum elements were utilized across the volume to capture complex out-of-plane torsional behaviors and shear stress gradients.Refinement Parameters: A global mesh size was established based on the primary geometry, while automated localized mesh refinement was systematically forced at the internal boundaries of the primary and secondary pivot bores.Fillet Gradients: Higher element density was assigned around the transition fillets joining the web to the cylindrical bosses. This high spatial mesh distribution guarantees an accurate capturing of high von Mises stress gradients, preventing artificial stiffness errors or stress divergence.

Boundary Conditions and Load Application
The numerical simulation environment replication adhered strictly to kinematic constraints:Restraint Definition (Clamp.1): A zero-displacement boundary condition ($Ux = Uy = Uz = Rotx = Roty = Rotz = 0$) was rigidly executed on the interior surface area of the primary base bore interface, locking the model securely.Loading Condition (Remote Load.1): A remote loading point was defined along the secondary moving pivot bore axis. While the directional linear resultant forces remained unspecified, a pure static Resultant Moment ($Z\text{-moment}$) equal to $100 \text{ N}\cdot\text{m}$ was introduced, subjecting the aerodynamic web to severe twisting and bending deformations.

PROTOTYPE MODEL 
The structural validation of the geometric model was accomplished within DS SIMULIA. The constituent material utilized for this validation run is Aluminum Alloy 1060, maintaining a certified 0.2% Proof (Yield) Stress of $2.89 \times 10^8 \text{ N/m}^2$ (289 MPa) and an Ultimate Tensile Strength (UTS) of $3.14 \times 10^8 \text{ N/m}^2$ (314 MPa)

INFERENCE
The numerical simulation executed in DS SIMULIA demonstrates that the compact linkage bracket achieves excellent structural performance under the designated $100 \text{ N}\cdot\text{m}$ moment load. The model reached a successful convergence status without computational errors.Based on the stress distribution analysis, the peak von Mises stress reached approximately 39 MPa, which sits well below the material's yield strength of 289 MPa. This yields a substantial factor of safety, proving that the bracket can safely transfer loading without risking plastic deformation or premature failure. The maximum physical displacement was tightly confined to 0.1247 mm, indicating superior structural rigidity along the connecting web profile.The integration of generous fillets at the boss-to-web junctions successfully deflected high stress concentrations away from critical sharp boundaries. This validates that the aerodynamic, smooth-flowing surface geometry provides optimized stress path mitigation, making the component fully eligible for physical manufacturing or additive prototyping.
